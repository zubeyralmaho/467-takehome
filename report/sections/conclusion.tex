\section{Conclusion}

This report now supports a clear interim pattern across the completed questions: representation quality matters more than model family alone. When pretrained contextual or generative models are available, they are the strongest finished systems in every mature comparison. At the same time, the results do not support a simple classical-versus-neural narrative, because lightweight classical baselines remain competitive and randomly initialized neural sequence models do not consistently outperform them.

For Question 1, DistilBERT is the best sentiment classifier on the matched IMDb comparison, but the gap over TF-IDF + SVM is modest relative to the much larger gap over the supervised-only BiLSTM. This indicates that the main gain comes from contextual pretraining rather than from using a neural architecture in the abstract. For Question 2, BERT again dominates the comparison and achieves the strongest overall and per-entity F1, while the feature CRF remains a strong non-transformer baseline and the BiLSTM-CRF underperforms both alternatives. For Question 3, the pretrained DistilBART summarizer outperforms TextRank on every exported lexical metric in the current capped comparison, suggesting that pretrained abstractive generation is more effective than purely extractive ranking for CNN/DailyMail-style summaries.

Question 5 is less complete, but even the current trigram baseline is informative. Its high perplexity and semantically unstable generations make the limitations of short-context count-based modeling explicit, which in turn motivates the neural language-model follow-up. Across Questions 1, 2, 3, and the current state of 5, the most defensible project-level claim is therefore that pretrained models provide the strongest empirical results, classical baselines remain essential reference points, and supervised neural models without strong pretraining or further tuning are not reliably superior.

The report is still provisional because Question 4 has not yet been integrated into the written analysis and Question 5 is currently documented as a baseline-first section. The final version of this conclusion should therefore be revisited after the machine translation results are stable and any later Q5 comparison is folded into the report. Until then, the completed sections already support the central comparative lesson of the project: the main performance gains come from representation quality and pretraining, not from adopting a neural architecture by itself.
