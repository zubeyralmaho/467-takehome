\section{Question 1: Representation Learning in Text Classification}

\subsection{Dataset Description}

Question 1 uses the IMDb movie-review sentiment benchmark \cite{maas2011imdb}, which contains 50{,}000 balanced reviews split into 25{,}000 original training examples and 25{,}000 original test examples. The project pipeline normally applies a stratified 80/20 split to the training portion, but the matched larger-budget comparison used a capped setting to keep the neural runs tractable on the available hardware. Concretely, 4{,}000 reviews were first sampled from the original training split and then divided into 3{,}200 training and 800 validation examples, while 2{,}000 reviews were sampled from the original test split for final evaluation. Because the label distribution is balanced, accuracy and macro-F1 move closely together and both are useful summary metrics.

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}

All Q1 models share the same base text-normalization pipeline: HTML tags are stripped, text is lowercased, non-alphanumeric characters are removed, whitespace is normalized, and documents are truncated to 256 tokens. TF-IDF and BiLSTM consume these normalized word-level sequences directly, whereas DistilBERT applies WordPiece tokenization after the shared cleaning stage.

The preprocessing sweep was intentionally run as a cheaper validation-only study on TF-IDF + Logistic Regression rather than as a full end-to-end rerun of every model. Table~\ref{tab:q1_preprocessing_results} shows that keeping stopwords with lowercasing and 50{,}000 features (Experiment B) tied with preserving case (Experiment D), both reaching validation macro-F1 0.8178. Since the lowercase configuration is simpler and aligns naturally with the uncased DistilBERT backbone, the final larger-budget experiments kept the lowercase + keep-stopwords setting.

\subsection{Models}

\paragraph{TF-IDF + Logistic Regression / SVM.}
The sparse baselines use unigram and bigram TF-IDF features with 50{,}000 maximum features and regularization strength \(C = 1.0\). Two classifiers were tested on top of the same representation: Logistic Regression as a probabilistic linear baseline and a linear SVM as a max-margin variant.

\paragraph{BiLSTM.}
The word-level neural baseline uses learned 128-dimensional embeddings, a single bidirectional LSTM with hidden size 128, dropout 0.3, batch size 64, and Adam optimization with learning rate \(10^{-3}\). Early stopping monitors validation macro-F1 with patience 2 and training can run for up to 6 epochs. In the current implementation, this model does not use pretrained GloVe initialization, so its representation quality depends entirely on the supervised IMDb subset.

\paragraph{DistilBERT.}
The contextual baseline fine-tunes \texttt{distilbert-base-uncased} \cite{sanh2019distilbert} with batch size 16, learning rate \(2 \times 10^{-5}\), weight decay 0.01, warmup ratio 0.1, and maximum sequence length 256. The completed larger-budget run converged within 2 epochs and produced the strongest validation and test scores among all finished Q1 models.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:q1_overall_results} summarizes the matched 4k/2k comparison. DistilBERT is the strongest finished model with test macro-F1 0.8793 and test accuracy 0.8795. It outperforms TF-IDF + SVM by roughly 0.028 macro-F1 points, TF-IDF + LR by roughly 0.039, and BiLSTM by roughly 0.178. The classical sparse baselines remain competitive, especially the linear SVM, while the randomly initialized BiLSTM trails substantially behind both TF-IDF variants and the transformer model.

\subsection{Error Analysis}

The exported misclassification artifacts indicate that negation remains the dominant failure mode across all representation families. For DistilBERT, 4 of the top 5 sampled test errors fall into the \texttt{negation\_handling} category, usually in long reviews where local positive phrases are later overturned by a negative judgment. The TF-IDF baselines show a broader mix of negation, mixed sentiment, and domain-specific ambiguity, which is consistent with lexical models that cannot fully resolve discourse-level polarity shifts. BiLSTM is the most brittle model: its test error summary is still dominated by negation, but it also struggles with very long reviews and mixed sentiment, and its confusion matrix shows a much stronger drop in positive recall than the other approaches.

This qualitative pattern matches the aggregate metrics. DistilBERT's contextual encoder is better at following sentiment reversals across long documents, TF-IDF remains surprisingly robust when the signal is lexical and local, and the supervised-only BiLSTM does not learn a representation strong enough to close the gap. In practical terms, the main unresolved Q1 errors are no longer simple vocabulary misses but cases where polarity depends on clause interaction, contrast, or delayed sentiment cues.

\subsection{Discussion}

The final larger-budget ranking for Question 1 is clear: DistilBERT is the strongest model, TF-IDF + SVM is the strongest lightweight baseline, TF-IDF + LR is close behind, and BiLSTM is the weakest of the finished runs. The most important takeaway is that representation choice matters more than small preprocessing differences on this task. The preprocessing sweep changed validation macro-F1 only marginally, whereas switching from sparse TF-IDF features to contextual transformer embeddings produced the largest reliable gain.

At the same time, the results do not support a naive "neural is always better" conclusion. Moving from sparse features to a randomly initialized word-level BiLSTM did not help; in fact, it substantially hurt performance on the matched budget. This makes the comparison more informative: the benefit comes from contextual pretraining rather than from replacing linear classifiers with a neural sequence model by itself. For the final report claim, DistilBERT should be presented as the best-performing Q1 model, TF-IDF + SVM as a strong and efficient baseline, and BiLSTM as evidence that dense representations without pretraining or further tuning are not sufficient on their own.
