\section{Question 4: Machine Translation}

\subsection{Dataset Description}

Question 4 uses the English-to-German Multi30k translation benchmark \cite{elliott2016multi30k}. In this project, the dataset is accessed through its Hugging Face mirror and treated as a short-caption translation task. That makes it a useful benchmark for testing whether a model can produce fluent target-language sentences while preserving compact visual descriptions from the source caption. The current Q4 evidence now includes two finished systems evaluated on the same capped 100-example validation and 100-example test splits: a pretrained transformer reference and a custom seq2seq-plus-attention baseline.

The comparison is direct at evaluation time but not perfectly budget-matched at training time. The transformer run keeps a 100-example train slice only for artifact-contract consistency and does not perform task-specific fine-tuning, whereas the seq2seq model is trained from scratch on 1000 capped training examples. This distinction matters for interpretation, so the section should be read as a practical pretrained-versus-classical comparison rather than a matched from-scratch study.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Model}

The stronger system uses the pretrained \texttt{Helsinki-NLP/opus-mt-en-de} machine-translation checkpoint \cite{tiedemann2020opusmt}, which is built on a transformer-style translation stack \cite{vaswani2017attention}. Source and target casing are preserved, inputs and decoded outputs are capped at 96 tokens, and generation uses beam search with 4 beams and length penalty 1.0. Instead of fine-tuning on Multi30k, this pipeline evaluates the checkpoint directly as a strong practical reference.

The classical comparator is a compact GRU encoder-decoder with additive attention \cite{bahdanau2015nmt}. It uses 96-dimensional token embeddings, a 128-dimensional hidden state, single-layer recurrent stacks, teacher forcing during training, and a maximum decoded length of 32 tokens. Like the transformer run, it preserves source and target casing, but unlike the pretrained model it must learn translation behavior from the capped 1000-example training split.

Evaluation uses BLEU and ChrF on both validation and test. BLEU gives a familiar n-gram precision view of translation quality, while ChrF is more tolerant of inflectional and morphological variation and is often informative for German output. Together, the two metrics provide a lightweight but credible baseline suite for the current stage of Q4.

\subsection{Results}

Table~\ref{tab:q4_overall_results} summarizes the finished comparison. The pretrained transformer reaches validation/test BLEU 0.4008/0.3572 and validation/test ChrF 0.6569/0.6380, while the custom seq2seq baseline reaches only 0.0396/0.0416 BLEU and 0.1885/0.1949 ChrF. On the shared validation/test evaluation, the transformer therefore leads the seq2seq model by 0.3613/0.3155 BLEU and 0.4684/0.4431 ChrF.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}

The qualitative examples reinforce the metric gap. On test example \texttt{test\_63}, the transformer exactly matches the reference translation for \textit{Two men playing guitar in front of a large audience.}, which is the ideal behavior for a short caption translation task. Even its weaker exported example, \texttt{test\_8}, remains understandable: it rewrites the running-man caption as a longer relative-clause construction that is less concise and less reference-like than the gold translation, but it still preserves most of the core meaning.

The seq2seq outputs show why the classical baseline remains far behind. Its best exported test example, \texttt{test\_34}, starts with the correct topic but degenerates into repetition in \textit{Zwei Männer in in einem in einer}. Its weakest exported example, \texttt{test\_31}, collapses to \textit{Mehrere Männer auf der}, which truncates the sentence before the central meaning is expressed. In other words, the seq2seq model sometimes captures local lexical cues, but it often fails to sustain grammatical completion or source-faithful content.

\subsection{Discussion}

The current Q4 evidence now supports a clearer conclusion than the earlier baseline-only draft. The pretrained transformer is the strongest finished translation system by a wide margin and should be treated as the main Q4 result, while the seq2seq model serves as the classical reference point that shows how difficult translation remains for small from-scratch models on limited data.

The remaining caveat is experimental alignment. Because the transformer is evaluated as a pretrained system rather than fine-tuned on Multi30k, a more budget-aligned next step would be either stronger seq2seq tuning or task-specific transformer fine-tuning on a shared train split. For the current report state, however, the direct comparison already answers the assignment question well enough to show that pretraining dominates the compact classical baseline on capped Multi30k.
